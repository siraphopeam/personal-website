I quit the agency and went in-house. Here's what changed.

After nine years of agency life — junior planner, senior, manager, director — I switched sides. The work, the politics, the metrics, even the pace of a Tuesday morning: almost everything is different.

The first week in-house was disorienting in a way I didn't expect. Nobody asked me for a tissue session. Nobody asked for a 60-slide deck. The number of meetings on my calendar dropped by half. And nobody — nobody — asked me what the "big idea" was.

I'd spent nine years training a particular set of muscles. Strategic planner muscles. Junior, senior, manager, director — the same job, just bigger decks and harder clients. I was good at it, mostly. But somewhere along the way, the gap between the strategy I shipped and the work that actually went out the door kept getting wider, and I stopped enjoying the part where I closed that gap with a PDF.

So I quit. Not in a dramatic way. I just… picked peace over the next promotion, took an in-house marketing manager role at an electronic-appliance brand, and started over.

Here's what's different so far.

1. Time moves differently

At the agency, every hour belonged to a code. There was always a timesheet, always a deadline, always five accounts pulling at the same calendar. The default speed was "fast", and "thoughtful" was an upgrade you had to fight for.

In-house, the metronome is different. The product takes weeks to ship. The customer doesn't change brand briefs every three months. A campaign isn't a flurry of decks and revisions — it's a small thing you put into the world, then watch.

The hardest adjustment wasn't the slower pace. It was learning to stay with a single problem long enough to actually fix it.

That sounds obvious. It isn't. After a decade of agency speed, sitting with one problem for two weeks feels like procrastination. It isn't. It's the work.

2. The metrics get real

At an agency, the closest you usually get to a number is the campaign report at the end. You see the click-through, you see the awareness lift, you see the case-study chart. And then you move on to the next pitch.

In-house, the numbers are the job. There's no client to hand them to. There's just you, the dashboard, and the question: did this thing actually work?

What I track now, every Monday: revenue by channel, blended CAC, retention by cohort, CRM open and click rates, a hand-written note about anything weird in the funnel. That's it. Six numbers and a paragraph.

Before, I'd have built that into a 30-slide read-out. Now it lives in a single page in Notion, and the team reads it in three minutes.

A note on dashboards

I used to be skeptical of marketers who say "data-driven" out loud. I'm still a little skeptical. But I've come to believe that most marketing teams aren't actually using data — they're just using charts. Charts are decoration. Data is when a number changes a decision you were about to make.

3. The politics flip

Agency politics are mostly about winning: winning the pitch, winning the brief, winning the relationship, winning the room. In-house politics are about shipping: getting the engineering team to take a marketing request seriously, getting finance to approve a test budget, getting sales to stop launching things on Friday afternoons.

Different game, different muscles. I'm worse at this game than I was at the agency one. But it's the game I'd rather practice.

4. Strategic planning, but smaller

At the agency, "strategy" meant a document. A long one. With a journey map, an audience archetype, a positioning statement, and a tagline you'd defend to the death.

In-house, strategy is just what we're going to do this quarter, and what we're going to ignore. One page. Three bets. No tagline, please.

  • Bet 1. The headline thing the team is trying to make happen.
  • Bet 2. The defensive thing — usually retention or efficiency.
  • Bet 3. The exploratory thing — small budget, fast, willing to fail.

If you've spent any time in agency strategy, you'll recognize this as roughly the same shape as a brief — minus the choreography.

5. The trade-offs are real

I'd be lying if I said I didn't miss anything. The agency was my school for ten years. I miss the variety. I miss working alongside ten other people who are obsessively good at one specific thing. I miss the energy of a great pitch.

What I don't miss: the timesheet, the Sunday-night dread, the feeling of pouring strategy into a deck that nobody on the production side will ever read.

If you're thinking about it

I get this question now, mostly from agency friends. Three honest things:

  1. You'll take a step backwards before you take one forwards. Your title might shrink. Your team will. Your scope, weirdly, will get bigger — but it'll feel smaller, because there's no client to impress.
  2. The first six months are about unlearning. You'll catch yourself building a 40-slide deck for a meeting that needed a Slack message. Forgive yourself; rewrite it as a Slack message; move on.
  3. You won't miss the chaos as much as you think. You'll miss the people. Stay in touch with the people.

I don't think in-house is "better" than agency in any universal sense. It's a different shape of job, with different rewards, attached to different kinds of stress. I picked it because I wanted to make real things move, and I wanted to do it slowly enough that the moving was actually mine.

So far, no regrets. Ask me again in a year.
